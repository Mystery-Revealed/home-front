# "The Home Front" — Build Specification
### Unit 7 Game · World War II Texas · *Common Build Standards apply (Pattern A — Engine Game)*

## 1. At a Glance
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **TEKS** | 7.7E (WWII's impact on Texas), 7.11A/B (industry and jobs — urbanization begins here), 7.17B (Oveta Culp Hobby) |
| **Role** | A Texas town's wartime civic coordinator, 1941–1945 (everyone plays the same town — class-wide group) |
| **Type** | Solo strategy — 6 phases × 2 decisions = **12 graded actions**; a town-status panel grows (base, plant, farms, main street) |
| **Adapter** | `homeFront.js` · meters `{ warEffort, community, fairness }` start 50 — **fairness** is the game's conscience meter |

**Pitch:** Pearl Harbor just changed everything, and your little Texas town is about to become part of the biggest effort in American history — an airfield on the old pasture, a plant hiring everyone who can rivet, and four years of doing your part. Run the home front, and run it fair.

**Teaching idea:** your outline's WWII paragraph, made playable: Texas as a **training-ground state** (wide-open spaces, mild climate), the war boom in **oil and aviation**, and Texans' contributions at home. The third meter carries the honest thread: the home front ran on the work of **women, Black Texans, and Mexican Americans** who were asked to give everything while being treated as less than equal — the game names it and grades fairness, planting the seed for the unit's civil-rights history (Hector García's veterans, 7.7D).

## 2. Content Bank
Texas hosted a huge share of wartime training: **~1.25 million+ trained** at Texas posts and airfields (Randolph "West Point of the Air," Fort Hood's tank destroyers, dozens more); the climate and space your outline cites are the *why* · industry: aviation plants (the great Fort Worth bomber plant), Gulf shipyards, and **oil for the Allies** (pipelines like the Big Inch run east) · daily home front: **rationing books** (sugar, gas, tires), scrap drives, victory gardens, war bonds · **women fill the plants** ("Rosie" era) and **Oveta Culp Hobby** of Houston leads the **Women's Army Corps** · Texas held **POW camps** (tens of thousands of German prisoners worked farms under the Geneva rules — a strange, true Texas story; humane treatment is the graded right) · Black and Mexican American Texans worked and served under segregation's rules — named plainly, dignity carried · war's end: veterans return, the **GI Bill**, and Hector García's **American GI Forum** fight (1948) begins the bridge to the civil-rights era.

## 3. Mechanics
Meters: **War Effort** ✈️ (training, production, drives), **Community** 🏘️ (housing, schools, morale in a town doubling overnight), **Fairness** ⚖️ (who shares the work and the credit). Ending = sum → tiers ("A Town That Answered" / "Did Our Part" / "Strained Seams") + debrief: WWII industrialized and urbanized Texas for good (7.11), and the people who powered the home front would spend the next decades winning the equality they'd earned — Unit 7's second half.

## 4. Sample Scripted Phase + Beat Matrix
**Phase 1 — The Base Comes (decision 1):** *The Army wants 2,000 acres of pasture for a flight-training field — and wants your town to say yes fast.*
- ✅ **Say yes and organize: fair land prices settled quickly, roads and water committed.** War Effort +15, Community +5. *"Texas won bases because towns like yours moved fast — flat land, flying weather, and a handshake that held. Over a million troops trained in Texas."*
- ⚠️ **Say yes, but let the land deals sort themselves out.** War Effort +5, Community −10. *"Speculators feasted where towns didn't organize."*
- ❌ **Bargain hard for months.** War Effort −10. *"The Army had a war on. The base went to the next county."*

**Phase 1 (decision 2):** *Two thousand trainees are coming to a town of three thousand.* ✅ Open homes, church halls, and a USO room — organized welcome (Community +15) · ⚠️ Let the boys figure it out (Community −5) · ❌ Price-gouge the rents (Fairness −15).

**Beat matrix:**
| Phase | Beat | Right answer teaches |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | The plant hires | Hire everyone who can do the work — women on the line at equal piece rates, Black and Mexican American workers in real jobs (✅ Fairness + War Effort; feedback: plants that did this out-produced the ones that didn't — and it still took fights to make it happen); a Hobby/WAC recruiting beat honors women in uniform |
| 3 | Rationing year | Run the ration board straight — same book, same rules, no favors for the mayor's cousin (✅ Fairness is literal here); victory gardens and scrap drives (✅ Community) |
| 4 | The POW camp | Treat prisoners by the rules — work, pay in scrip, decent food (✅ the real Texas record; feedback: farmers and prisoners often parted with respect; some POWs later returned as immigrants) |
| 5 | Oil and the long haul (1944) | Keep the pipelines and the double shifts going through war-weariness — rest rotations, honest news, war-bond rallies that tell the truth (✅ endurance beat) |
| 6 | Victory and after | V-J Day: joy, and then the harder question — jobs and respect for *all* the returning veterans; back the GI Bill office and the town's Black and Mexican American vets using it (✅ the bridge beat; García's American GI Forum is named in the debrief) |
